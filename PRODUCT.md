# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Academic researchers: PhD students, postdocs, and PIs in university and research institute labs. They are comfortable with GenBank files, primer design, restriction enzyme cloning, and Sanger sequencing. They use Ori at a bench computer or laptop during active experiments, often under time pressure. They have enough tool fluency to appreciate precision but not enough patience for software that fights them.

## Product Purpose

Ori is an open-source, browser-based molecular biology workbench. It exists to give academic researchers a credible, free alternative to SnapGene: circular and linear sequence maps, restriction enzyme analysis, gel digest simulation, primer design with real thermodynamics, Sanger alignment and chromatogram viewing, ORF detection, AI co-pilot, and cloning simulation. Success means a researcher never reaches for SnapGene because Ori does the job better and without a license fee.

## Brand Personality

Expert confidence with warmth. Scholarly but not cold. The tool a thoughtful, careful scientist would build for themselves: precise, readable, unhurried. Three words: **rigorous, legible, open**.

The "Manuscript palette" already captures this: parchment backgrounds, deep forest green, amber, Playfair Display headings, paper grain texture, ruled lines. This is a high-quality lab notebook, not a SaaS dashboard.

The open-source identity matters. Ori should feel welcoming to a first-year grad student who finds SnapGene intimidating, while being credible enough for a PI who has been doing cloning for 20 years.

## Anti-references

- **SnapGene**: clinical, Windows-native, feature-dense, visually dated. Ori replaces it; it must not resemble it.
- **Benchling**: corporate enterprise SaaS, heavy blue palette, overcrowded, built for teams not individuals. Too much hierarchy, too much chrome.
- **Generic biotech dark mode**: cyan-on-black terminal aesthetic, neon accents, overdesigned for engineers not biologists.
- **Generic life-science startup beige**: bland warm-neutral SaaS with no point of view. Characterless. Ori has a distinct aesthetic; do not sand it down.

## Design Principles

1. **The lab notebook standard.** Every surface should feel like a well-kept scientific record. Not a consumer app, not an enterprise dashboard. Precision is visible. Careful work shows.

2. **Earn the serif.** Playfair Display and the manuscript palette carry authority. Don't dilute them with casual UI patterns. Every component should feel worthy of the typography.

3. **Readable over impressive.** Sequence data, primer statistics, thermodynamic values, and chromatogram traces are the product. Decoration earns its place only when it aids comprehension.

4. **Open-source warmth.** Forest green over corporate blue. Warmth over clinical whiteness. The UI should feel inviting to a student as much as a veteran scientist.

5. **Two registers, one visual system.** The landing page is brand register (the design IS the argument for using Ori). Everything inside the app shell is product register (the design SERVES the scientific work). Both pull from the same manuscript palette but at different expressive intensities.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA. Keyboard navigability for all interactive elements. Sufficient contrast ratios throughout the manuscript palette (the warm neutrals require care; verify parchment-on-text pairs). No motion-only affordances; respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. Color is never the sole indicator of meaning in sequence annotations or primer quality scores.
